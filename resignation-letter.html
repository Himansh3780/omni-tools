How to Write a Professional Resignation Letter

Resigning from a job can be stressful, but writing the letter shouldn't be. A resignation letter is a formal document that officially begins the process of your departure. It is kept in your employment file, so it is crucial to keep it professional, positive, and brief.

What should be included?

  • The Statement: Clearly state that you are resigning. Do not be vague.
  • The Date: Specify your last day of work (usually 2 weeks from the date of the letter).
  • The Gratitude: Thank the employer for the opportunities you had. This burns no bridges.
  • The Offer: Offer to help with the transition or training of your replacement.

Templates Explained

1. Formal (Standard): Best for corporate environments. It sticks to the facts and keeps emotion to a minimum.
2. Friendly (Warm): Use this if you have a good relationship with your manager and want to express genuine appreciation.
3. Short (Basic): Use this if you want to keep it strictly legal and avoid giving any reasons for leaving.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How much notice should I give?
The standard professional courtesy is two weeks. However, check your employment contract, as some senior roles require a month or more.

Q: Should I say why I am leaving?
No. You are not legally required to give a reason, and it is often better to leave it out of the written letter. Save the details for the exit interview if you wish.
